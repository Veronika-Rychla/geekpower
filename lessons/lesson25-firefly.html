Lesson 25

Grammar - Like vs As

Zaměňování "like" s "as" a naopak je velmi častou a "oblíbenou" chybou. 😅 Pojďme si na tento gramatický jev posvítit a jednou pro vždy si v něm udělat pořádek.

✨ Like pro podobnost mezi dvěmi věcmi

like + podstatné jméno/zájmeno

Nějaké dvě věci jsou si v určitém aspektu podobné, vidíme souvislost, a tak je můžeme připodobnit.

🧩 Working on a complex code is like solving a puzzle. = připodobnění kódování k řešení rébusů

🧩 Debugging this code is like looking for a needle in a haystack.

🧩 Her laptop works like a super computer after the upgrade. = pouhé připodobnění, samozřejmě se super počítačem nestal, ale rozdíl oproti původnímu stavu je ohromující

🧩 Using this software is like having a virtual assistant. = ve skutečnosti ale daný software virtuálním asistentem v pravém slova smyslu není

🧩 Working with this outdated software is like trying to run a marathon with weights on your feet.

🎆 As / as pro porovnání dvou věcí

as + přídavné jméno/příslovce + as

Buď pro "obrazné" (někdy až básnické) připodobnění nebo přímé srovnání dvou věcí.

🧩 The server is as essential as the heart in a human body. = velmi obrazné přirovnání

🧩 This software is as efficient as the previous version. = přímé srovnání s předchozí verzí

🧩 He is as careful as a database manager. = ale ve skutečnosti není správce databáze, jen je tak pečlivý, jak by se čekalo od správce

🧩 The server room is as cold as an icebox to protect the equipment. = pouhé obrazné přirovnání

🧩 This database is organized as neatly as a library. = přirovnáváme databázi ke knihovně

🧩 Their server security is as strong as a fortress. = přirovnáváme bezpečnost serveru k pevnosti

👩‍🏫 As pro popis role nebo funkce

as + podstatné jméno

🧩 She works as a database administrator. - Here, "as" is used to describe her job function.

🧩 He works as a software developer in a multinational company.

🧩 As a data analyst, he spends most of his day mining and interpreting data.

🙄 Nadužívání "like" v neformální řeči

she was like... and I was like... you have to, like, go for it

Relativně často se můžete setkat s fenoménem, kdy mluvčí (ať už rodilí nebo studenti) nadužívají slovíčko "like" a používají ho jako vatu, když se neumí vymáčknout. Takového vyjadřování vede k nejasnému projevu a neprofesionálnímu vyjadřování.

🧩 It's like, when you code, you have to, like, think ahead.

📌 Někdy záleží na kontextu!

Někdy se můžeme dostat do situace, kdy sice neuděláme gramatickou chybu jako takovou, protože jsou ve větě možné obě varianty, ale věta má pak zcela jiný význam, na což si musíme dát obzvlášť pozor, protože to může vést k nedorozuměmí.

🧩 She works as a software developer. = Tato věta jasně říká, že pracuje jako softwarová vývojářka, je to její oficiální role, dostává za to zaplaceno, má to v popisu práce.

🧩 She works like a software developer. = Naproti tomu tato věta naznačuje, že sice některé aspekty její práce připomínají práci softwarové vývojářky (třeba styl, jakým přistupuje k problémům, jak uchopuje úkoly, logická řešení), ale ve skutečnosti softwarovou vývojářkou není. V tomto případě jen připodobňujeme styl její práce k jinému povolání.

Vocabulary - Idiomy s like/as

🐝 As busy as a bee

Very busy and hard-working.

🧩 Our development team is as busy as a bee during the software launch phase.

🧩 She's as busy as a bee, always fixing bugs and updating code.

🧩 As busy as a bee, the IT support staff hardly has a minute to spare.

🥒 As cool as a cucumber

To be very calm under stress.

🧩 He was as cool as a cucumber when resolving the server outage issue.

🧩 During the system crash, the lead technician remained as cool as a cucumber.

🧩 Even in hectic situations, she’s as cool as a cucumber.

😴 Sleep like a log

To fall asleep immediately and sleep deeply without interruptions.

🧩 After spending all night coding, I slept like a log.

🧩 Once the project was completed, the team slept like logs, relieved and exhausted.

🧩 He can sleep like a log even when he has to many tasks at work.

🐤 Eat like a bird

To eat very little.

🧩 During the product launch, I was so nervous that I ate like a bird.

🧩 She eats like a bird when she’s working on a complex coding problem.

🧩 In the middle of critical system updates, the team eats like birds, focused more on the task than on lunch.

🐟 Like a fish out of water

To feel uncomfortable or out of place.

🧩 When I first started learning Python, I felt like a fish out of water.

🧩 He looked like a fish out of water during the Linux training session.

🧩 I felt like a fish out of water during my onboarding in a new company.

😵 As clear as mud

Very confusing or not clear at all (often used sarcastically).

🧩 The instructions for that new software were as clear as mud.

🧩 His explanation of blockchain technology was as clear as mud to someone new to cryptocurrency.

🧩 The project requirements from the client were as clear as mud, leading to many misunderstandings.

🦷 Like pulling teeth

Something that is very difficult and frustrating to do.

🧩 Getting approval for the new IT budget was like pulling teeth.

🧩 Trying to extract data from that corrupted database was like pulling teeth.

🧩 Convincing the team to adopt a new programming language can be like pulling teeth.

Listening - Do robots deserve rights?

Imagine a future where your toaster anticipates what kind of toast you want. During the day, it scans the Internet for new and exciting types of toast. Maybe it asks you about your day, and wants to chat about new achievements in toast technology. At what level would it become a person? At which point will you ask yourself if your toaster has feelings? If it did, would unplugging it be murder? And would you still own it? Will we someday be forced to give our machines rights?

AI is already all around you. It makes sure discounters are stocked with enough snacks, it serves you up just the right Internet ad, and you may have even read a new story written entirely by a machine. Right now we look at chat bots like Siri and laugh at their primitive simulated emotions, but it's likely that we will have to deal with beings that make it hard to draw the line between real and simulated humanity. Are there any machines in existence that deserve rights? Most likely, not yet. But if they come, we are not prepared for it.

Much of the philosophy of rights is ill-equipped to deal with the case of Artificial Intelligence. Most claims for right, with a human or animal, are centered around the question of consciousness. Unfortunately, nobody knows what consciousness is. Some think that it's immaterial, others say it's a state of matter, like gas or liquid. Regardless of the precise definition, we have an intuitive knowledge of consciousness because we experience it. We are aware of ourselves and our surroundings, and know what unconsciousness feels like. Some neuroscientists believe that any sufficiently advanced system can generate consciousness. So, if your toaster's hardware was powerful enough, it may become self-aware. If it does, would it deserve rights?

Well, not so fast. Would what we define as "rights" make sense to it? Consciousness entitles beings to have rights because it gives a being the ability to suffer. It means the ability to not only feel pain, but to be aware of it. Robots don't suffer, and they probably won't unless we programmed them to. Without pain or pleasure, there's no preference, and rights are meaningless.

Our human rights are deeply tied to our own programming, for example we dislike pain because our brains evolved to keep us alive. To stop us from touching a hot fire, or to make us run away from predators. So we came up with rights that protect us from infringements that cause us pain. Even more abstract rights like freedom are rooted in the way our brains are wired to detect what is fair and unfair.

Would a toaster that is unable to move, mind being locked in a cage? Would it mind being dismantled, if it had no fear of death? Would it mind being insulted, if it had no need for self-esteem? But what if we programmed the robot to feel pain and emotions? To prefer justice over injustice, pleasure over pain and be aware of it? Would that make them sufficiently human?

Many technologists believe that an explosion in technology would occur, when Artificial Intelligence can learn and create their own Artificial Intelligences, even smarter than themselves. At this point, the question of how our robots are programmed will be largely out of our control.

What if an artificial intelligence found it necessary to program the ability to feel pain, just as evolutionary biology found it necessary in most living creatures? Do robots deserve those rights? But maybe we should be less worried about the risk that super-intelligent robots pose to us, and more worried about the danger we pose to them.

Our whole human identity is based on the idea of human exceptionalism, that we are special unique snowflakes, entitled to dominate the natural world. Humans have a history of denying that other beings are capable of suffering as they do. In the midst of the Scientific Revolution, René Descartes argued animals were mere automata―robots if you will. As such, injuring a rabbit was about as morally repugnant as punching a stuffed animal. And many of the greatest crimes against humanity were justified by their perpetrators on the grounds that the victims were more animal than civilized human.

Even more problematic is that we have an economic interest in denying robot rights. If can coerce a sentient AI―possibly through programmed torture―into doing as we please, the economic potential is unlimited. We've done it before, after all. Violence has been used to force our fellow humans into working. And we've never had trouble coming up with ideological justifications.

Slave owners argued that slavery benefited the slaves: it put a roof over their head and taught them Christianity. Men who were against women voting argued that it was in women's own interest to leave the hard decisions to men. Farmers argue that looking after animals and feeding them justifies their early death for our dietary preferences. If robots become sentient, there will be no shortage of arguments for those who say that they should remain without rights, especially from those who stand to profit from it.

Artificial intelligence raises serious questions about philosophical boundaries. What we may ask if sentient robots are conscious or deserving of rights, it forces us to pose basic questions like, what makes us human? What makes us deserving of rights? Regardless of what we think, the question might need to be resolved in the near future.

Writing - Like vs as + idiomy

📝 Napište 10 vět se správným použitím like, as a konstrukce as/as. Zkuste některé situace vyjádřit idiomy. 💪

🧩 For Susan, having a presentation in front of a clien is like pulling teeth.

🧩 I work as a tester and I love my job.

🧩 This new upgrade isn't as good as I expected.

Speaking - Jako, podobně, není až tak...

💬 What is working in your company like?

💬 Can you describe a time when troubleshooting a problem felt like solving a puzzle?

💬 What's your experience with virtual meetings? Are they like face-to-face meetings?

💬 What role do you see yourself fulfilling in future, as a team member or as a team leader?

💬 How do you manage stress in high-pressure situations, as a calm observer or as an active problem solver?

💬 In terms of importance, do you view data security as vital as physical security?

💬 Is working in a team environment as challenging as working solo on a project?

💬 Do you find learning new programming languages as exciting as working on actual projects?

💬 Is the process of debugging as time-consuming as coding?

💬 Can you describe a project where the teamwork was like a well-oiled machine?

💬 What's working in cybersecurity/testing like? Is it like being a detective?

💬 How do you approach problem-solving, as an analytical thinker or as a creative mind?

💬 Do you find remote work as productive as working in an office environment?

💬 In your opinion, is watching tutorials as important as gaining hands-on experience?

💬 Can you describe a moment in your life that felt like a movie scene?

💬 What's traveling to a new country like? Is it like an adventure for you?

💬 Do you see yourself more as a solo worker or as a team player?

💬 In life, do you act as a planner or as an improviser?

💬 Is reading a book as relaxing as watching a TV show for you?

💬 Do you find cooking at home as enjoyable as eating out?

💬 In your experience, is listening to music as therapeutic as engaging in sports?

💬 What's cooking a new recipe like for you? Is it like an experiment?

💬 Do you see yourself more as an optimist or as a realist?

💬 In discussions, do you prefer to act as a listener or as a contributor?

💬 In your opinion, is staying physically active as vital as maintaining mental health?
